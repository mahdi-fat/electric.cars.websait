History of electric and gasoline engines The first practical electric cars were introduced in the 1880s. Electric cars were common in the late 19th century and early 20th century, until the production of such cars suffered a sharp decline with the development of internal combustion engines and especially the electric start of the car and the mass production of cheap gasoline cars.
Structure of hybrid engines Generally, in hybrid cars, there is an electric motor that gets its electric energy from the battery. On the other side is a small internal combustion engine that either directly powers the wheels, or charges the battery through a generator.

__Electric Cars: A Growing Trend

__Electric Cars in the United States

Electric cars are becoming more popular in the United States. In 2018, the number of electric cars on the road was 1.18 million, up from 47,000 in 2011. The number of electric cars is expected to increase to 18.7 million by 2030.

__Electric Cars in China

China is the world's largest market for electric cars. In 2018, the number of electric cars on the road was 2.3 million, up from 1.2 million in 2017. The number of electric cars is expected to increase to 20 million by 2030.

__Electric Cars in Europe

Europe is the second largest market for electric cars. In 2018, the number of electric cars on the road was 1.2 million, up from 0.4 million in 2014. The number of electric cars is expected to increase to 17 million by 2030.

__the future of electric cars

The importance of electric cars in terms of environment, economy and energy has caused many countries of the world to plan to expand the use of these cars in the not-so-distant future. For example, in many European countries, regulations have been established to phase out gasoline and diesel vehicles by the end of 2030. Norway is a leader in this field and has planned to stop the production of gasoline cars in 2025.
